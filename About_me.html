About me
No, I have never watched a James Bond movie. I'm more of a TV show guy anyway. Nonetheless, my name is Daniel Wu, and I am a student studying Computer Science at NYU Tandon School of Engineering
Actually, I'm not technically a CS major yet because I can't switch majors until the end of the semester. If NYU's database could talk and tell only truth, then it would say that I am a Biomolecular Science major. So you might be asking, why the change in major?
Glad you asked. You might want to get some popcorn first.
I used to want to be a doctor. I volunteered at hospitals, partook in a selective Biomedical Sciences Academy throughout high school where I took multiple classes in Anatomy, Research, etc. through Rutgers University.
But in senior year, I didn't want to be pre-med, and I sure as heck did not want to go to medical school. Why did this happen? I'm not exactly sure but I am sure of what I felt.
So, I decided to give research a try and joined Alesha Castillo's lab at NYU Rory Meyers College of Nursing at the beginning of freshman year of college. I really enjoyed not only the wet lab work, but also the people, making my experience extremelly positive. Towards the end of the year, I interned at Dr. Castillo's lab through the NYU Tandon Undergraduate Summer Research Program.
Whoo! There's nothin' like summer in the city! Someone in a rush next to someone lookin' pretty! Not exactly. Going into the lab everyday became difficult, as I lost interest in doing wet lab work. What now?
A lot of my friends were computer science majors, so the topic has been in the back of my mind for a while. I enjoyed the singular Python course I took and have always enjoyed math, having already taken Calc 3, Linear Algebra, and Differential Equations at this point. Why not give it a shot? Maybe it's for me.
I noticed a part of the procedure the lab followed that required a human to decide whether a sample was good enough to keep. But "good enough" is a grey area and is completely subjective. In a field that completely determines the science of the future, subjectivity is the 2nd worst enemy (1st being a lack of funding). So, I asked Dr. Castillo if I could work on an algorithm that could make this subjective process objective.
I found this project extremely excited, spending hours coding up a preliminary algorithm and learning libraries, methods, and functions I have never heard of before. I would work at the lab, and I would work on it when I got home. Though for work-life balance, I eventually worked on it only when I was at the lab. With this, I was excited getting out of bed, only needing one alarm to fully wake up instead of a couple. (This project can be seen in the Projects Tab, along with my other projects.)
I knew I wanted to be a CS major, but there's a small issue. If I am swtiching to CS, I will be 2 semesters behind my peers as the CS class I took is not counted in the CS major's curriculum. So, I used online resources and my friends to help me self-study the first 2 intro CS classes. I adjusted very quickly to balancing studying, working, and more studying with having fun/exploring the city. Ultimately, I was able to test-out of the first intro course. There does not exist an option to test out of the 2nd, but the course is easier because of my self-study. I am currently self-studying the 3rd intro course and learning as much as I can.
So... yea... that's basically it for now. Interested? Check out the side bar for more info!
